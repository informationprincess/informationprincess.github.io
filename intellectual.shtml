Baseline

When I hear the word “intellectual”, my interest is piqued. While I can conceptually understand why the term has some negative associations, I’ve always wanted to be an intellectual. Not the cold-blooded stereotype who studies reality from afar. But a compassionate intellectual, who perceives others emotions, creatively reasons out problems and reflects on the impact of her actions. What else is the mind for if not to be used well?

My desire to share and learn cooperatively has led me to connect with others in the library field for lengthy discussions of issues, both face to face and through online communities. At a library supervisors meeting, I spoke on diversity, incorporating experiences in Deaf culture and my knowledge of American Sign Language. As a student teacher, I took the initiative to create and present a media literacy unit for the school’s 6th grade classes. My goal was to help students become critically thinking consumers of mass media.

My Development

As the time neared for me to take the required Research Methods course, I was full of nervous excitement. This class mattered greatly to me. I’d always been interested in research. Research has the potential to change the direction of a society and improve individuals’ quality of life. I remember getting sidetracked during my undergraduate years because I wanted to read the research more than write the paper. But I hadn’t had the opportunity to be involved in a formal research process. I considered this course to be a personal test of sorts. Would I really enjoy being a researcher or would I rather just read someone else’s results?

I wanted the study I conducted in class to be of use to the professional community. In the months before the course, I began to ponder librarian’s responses to certain genres of fiction. It occurred to me that some of the highest selling and circulating genres such as romance and some mysteries were generally disdained when librarians talked amongst themselves. I wanted to know if the personal taste of a librarian would have an impact on the reader’s advisory services they provided to patrons. As soon as the quarter began, I let my classmates know about my research interests. Fortunately, three other students were willing to sign on: Stephanie Hall, Emily Inlow and Heather Tucker. Our instructor, Dr. Hazel Taylor, helped us focus on what progress we could make on our research question in a single academic quarter.

We decided to conduct a pilot study by surveying librarians and library staff about their personal reading preferences as well as some questions about their reader’s advisory work. When I mentioned the project on an email discussion group of librarians I participate in, I received several inquiries and requests for the results. I knew to publish the results, I would need approval from Human Subjects Division, but I was also aware it was a lengthy process rarely attempted for student course projects. My group felt it important to be able to share our results and we worked to make our project pieces ready in time. With help from Dr. Taylor and Dr. Alpha DeLap, iSchool Director of Research Services, I gathered the requisite information and we received approval prior to launching our survey.

We had much interest and support from the professional community for our survey. In the end, we received 225 responses which was a bit overwhelming to analyze in our course time frame. We were able to analyze and write about the quantitative results and report on a subset of the qualitative data. The result was an interesting paper, but one that I’m eager to revise. For most of our group it was our first attempt at writing a research paper and Dr. Taylor gave us much useful feedback we have yet to incorporate. My classmates have agreed to remain in touch and help me rewrite the paper after I have conducted analysis on the entire set of qualitative data. In particular, I will be looking for patterns between the repondents personal preferences, the level of training in reader's advisory and the self-reported steps taken when performing reader's advisory services. When the process is complete, we want to submit the paper for publication to a journal such as Reference and User Services Quarterly. We have also considered submitting a summary article of our findings to a practitioner’s publication like Public Libraries. After that, I am interested in working with another researcher to follow up this study with focus groups and field observations to compare what is reported by librarians and what happens in practice. After this research project, I feel confident that I can conduct research and enjoy it.

Additional Experiences

  • For Dr. Matt Saxton, I completed “Secret Patron” surveys for the Seattle Public Library Reference Services Model Evaluation. Also under his direction, I conducted interviews of SPL Central staff for “Library as Place” research.
  • Over the last two years I’ve written several booklists as a member of the Sno-Isle Libraries Reader’s Advisory Team.
  • I have written reviews of teen literature for the Washington State Young Adult Review Group (WASHYARG), a regional reviewing organization of school and public librarians and other individuals interested in books, magazines, films, cassettes, videos, and other materials appropriate for adolescent use and intent.
